## Schematic models in the environmental sciences

In many areas of atmospheric and environmental physics, it's common to use
simplified ODE or PDE models to represent "schematic" views of physical
phenomena. Perhaps the best known example of this is the <Wikipedia>Lorenz
system</Wikipedia> (first animation below), which started out as a model of
atmospheric convection, but has become *the* paradigmatic example of a
low-dimensional ODE system exhibiting chaos.

Even in the absence of chaos, the methods of dynamical systems theory can be
useful for understanding the stability of states of physical systems as
parameters and conditions change. The second animation below shows the normal
form of a <Wikipedia>Hopf bifurcation</Wikipedia> as the bifurcation parameter
varies. For negative μ, there is a single stable equilibrium point at the
origin, while for positive μ, the equilibrium point becomes unstable and a
stable periodic limit cycle exists instead. Many physical systems acquire
oscillatory behavior through these kinds of bifurcations.

These kinds of low-dimensional ODE systems can be used to build PDE models of
extended media, like the example shown in the third animation. This is a
<Wikipedia>reaction-diffusion</Wikipedia> equation based on the
<Wikipedia>FitzHugh-Nagumo model</Wikipedia>. For different model parameter
values, it displays widely varying pattern formation and wave propagation
phenomena.

## Cloud and contrail physics

This approach seems to be less commonly used by researchers in cloud physics
and contrail modeling. The reasons are pretty clear: if you are looking for
maximum physical fidelity, a low-dimensional ODE model is not appropriate.
However, what these models can give you is some understanding of the competing
influences on physical phenomena, and they can give you an idea of the sources
of instability in systems.

I have found a few papers that build these kinds of very simplified models of
some cloud and contrail systems, and I think it would be an interesting avenue
to explore. First there is <Cite>berton-2023</Cite>, which has a
low-dimensional model of the formation of <Wikipedia>cirrus
uncinus</Wikipedia> ("mares' tails") clouds. They find that their model has
what looks like a stable equilibrium for some parameter values, and
oscillatory behavior for other conditions. Sounds like a Hopf bifurcation?
Maybe? Worth a look anyway.

Second, there is <Cite>kaercher-corcos-2025</Cite>, which has a different
low-dimensional model for different conditions, including some additional
physical processes and a different set of "important" couplings. I'm not 100%
sure what to do with this one yet, but it would be quite interesting to
compare it directly to Berton's model.

## Research plan

1. Duplicate the numerical results of <Cite>berton-2023</Cite>.
2. Perform a symbolic analysis of Berton's model to work out where the
   oscillations come from.
3. Duplicate the numerical results from <Cite>kaercher-corcos-2025</Cite>.
4. Perform some symbolic analysis of Kärcher & Corcos's model.
5. Maybe build a spatially extended version of Berton's model including
   something like the entrainment mechanism in Kärcher's model to see if it
   can replicate the "puffiness" of cirrus unicus, which looks like some sort
   of lateral inhibition effect.

Alongside actually doing some of this research, we'll try to explore how AI
tools alter the practice of research, especially when the work includes
programming, mathematical exposition, data analysis, and iterative writing.
