I started off using the same integration method as in the paper to get a good
baseline for validation against the paper's results (see [last
entry](../berton-2023-replication/)). However, the integration method used in
the paper (an explicit Euler method) isn't very good. It requires a very small
timestep (0.02-0.04 seconds), so a very large number of steps (900,000 for the
oscillatory case in the paper!). The paper makes a comment about the method
being stable because of the timestep choice, but that's more a comment on the
bad choice of integration method than anything else.

For future experiments, I wanted to use a better method. The model is stiff:
the timescale of the oscillations is around 10 hours, while the damping
coefficient $k$ is around 20 s⁻¹, corresponding to a timescale of around 0.05
s, giving a stiffness ratio of 720,000. This is why Berton's integrator only
works for very small timesteps, and a stiff solver is almost guaranteed to do
a better job.

The [Scipy `solve_ivp`
function](https://docs.scipy.org/doc/scipy/reference/generated/scipy.integrate.solve_ivp.html#scipy.integrate.solve_ivp)
gives access to a range of solvers. I took a look at the Radau and BDF methods
(fairly common methods for stiff system) and LSODA, which is a sophisticated
"comprehensive" ODE stepper with automatic stiffness detection and method
switching. I kept the original explicit Euler method for comparison.

## Fixed-output comparisons

The first comparison to do is to force the Scipy integrators to produce output
points at the same time resolution as the fixed timestep of the explicit Euler
method. The Scipy methods normally select variable timesteps themselves based
on solution smoothness and tolerance parameters, but it's possible to get
`solve_ivp` to run a post-processing step on the solution to produce equally
spaced output points.

The plots here show solutions for the steady and oscillatory test cases for
the original explicit Euler method and the Radau, BDF and LSODA integrators
from Scipy. Three important physical quantities (vertical position $z$,
crystal mass $m$ and vertical velocity $\dot{z}$) are plotted for each case
and each integrator.

## Native solver output times

As noted above, the Scipy integrators do not naturally produce output points
at a fixed timestep. The comparison in the previous section is there simply to
do a like-for-like comparison with the explicit Euler method. For real
applications, we will use the natural output times from the Scipy integrators.
These are generally much sparser that the Δt = 0.02s output of the explicit
Euler method.

The plots below show the same trajectories as above for the Scipy integrators,
but here we show exactly the output points the solvers produce to give some
idea of this sparseness: a few hundred output points instead of 900,000 for
the oscillatory case.

## Integration time

The timings here are approximate wall-clock times. For the Scipy integrators,
each bar is the median of three full simulations. For the explicit Euler
method, the times are projected from shorter calculations, because the method
is very slow. A logarithmic $y$-axis is needed to be able to display the
explicit Euler timings alongside the Scipy integrator times.

For the Scipy integrators, the fixed-output integrations have to do more work
just because of the final fixed timestep interpolation. Otherwise, the most
striking thing here is the *enormous* difference between the explicit Euler
method and the Scipy integrators: this is all down to the stiffness of the
model and the requirement to use a tiny timestep with the Euler method.

From these results, either the BDF or LSODA methods would be a good choice for
future experiments.
