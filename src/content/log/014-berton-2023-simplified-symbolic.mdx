This session looks like nothing at all, but there was some interesting stuff
going on here.

## The idea

The idea here was to do some symbolic analysis of a simplified version of the
Berton 2023 model to see if there might be a transition from the steady state
to the oscillatory solution in the form of a Hopf bifurcation. This whole idea
came out of a [long chat with
Claude](https://claude.ai/share/6f7d1922-e6cf-41fb-81db-531a4fa0b85c), where
I've been asking Opus 4.8 to look at the Berton 2023 model and another one, to
get some idea of what I could do with it.

The whole process of doing this is kind of overwhelming: Opus is *so* very
confident in everything it says, it can produce walls of algebra in seconds,
and it looks superficially all very convincing. (This is obviously me getting
my excuses in early. I didn't spot the error that Opus made, even though it
was pretty elementary.)

## The lesson

However (here's the click bait), it turns out that, left to its own devices,
*Opus is not very good at algebra*!!! It made a sign error in the calculation
of the Jacobian of the simplified system, which completely tipped everything
upside down. See the screen capture from Claude at the bottom, where three
minus signs turn into zero minus signs, through the magic of LLMs...

The lesson here, and it's one I probably needed to learn and that we all need
to be aware of: do not trust an LLM to do algebraic manipulations. It's as
likely (or more likely) to make silly errors than you are yourself. The only
difference is that it can make sign errors *much* more quickly than you can!

## Doing it the right way

LLMs are better at writing code, so instead of getting Claude to do the
algebra itself, I got it to write a [project
brief](/ian/log/additional/berton_3d_hopf_briefing/) for an agent to implement
the symbolic analysis in Python using
[Sympy](https://www.sympy.org/en/index.html). This is way more reliable and
the analysis worked, producing a [detailed
report](/ian/log/additional/berton_3d_hopf_analysis_summary/).

This is really another repetition of the "let the agent write code" point.
It's much easier for the agent to do that, producing a persistent artifact
that can be run repeatedly and modified to improve it, than it is for the
agent to "one shot" a calculation "by hand" (it really uses that phrase to
describe its calculations in the chat, which is quite weird).

*In the next episode of this exciting story, I'll use a different method on
the full Berton 2023 model to show that the approximation used in making the
simplified model was too much of a simplification, and there isn't a Hopf
bifurcation after all. But that was all quite complicated and is a story for
another day.*
